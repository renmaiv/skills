Goal: Quickly decide whether an arXiv paper is likely worth serious attention or probably sloppy / low‑value, using lightweight checks you can apply in a few minutes. Be chill about language, not overly excited about reccomendations.

1. Author & Venue Signal 

Ask: “Who is saying this, and where do they usually publish?”

Scan:

- Author names: search them on Google Scholar / Semantic Scholar.

- Track record: do they have multiple papers in relevant, recognizable venues (NeurIPS, ICML, ACL, top field journals, or at least consistent workshop / conference presence)?

- Affiliation: are they from a group or lab that appears elsewhere in the literature?

If: no publication history, no clear affiliation, or only vanity venues → risk of slop goes up.

2. Literature Fit 

Ask: “Does this live in the real conversation of the field?”

Scan:

- References: do they cite core classic work and recent relevant papers, or is it mostly self‑citations, random books, or unrelated stuff?

- Positioning: in the intro, do they situate their contribution against clear baselines (“Compared to X, we…”) or act like they’ve invented a whole domain?

If: few serious references, no clear prior art, grandiose claims → probable crank / low‑quality.

3. Argument and Method Quality 

Ask: “Does the paper argue like an adult?”

Skim:

- Abstract + Intro: clear problem statement, assumptions, and claimed contribution?

- Main method / theorem / experiment section: are steps explicit, or full of hand‑waving (“obvious”, “clearly follows”) where it’s not?

- If empirical: are datasets standard, baselines reasonable, metrics appropriate, ablations at least minimally sane?

If: key steps are skipped, assumptions hidden, or experiments feel cherry‑picked or opaque → downgrade.

4. Community Reaction & Follow‑through 

Ask: “Did anyone else care?”

Check:

- Citations: does it have thoughtful citations in other non‑slop work, or is it ignored / only cited by other noise?

- Versions: is there a newer version fixing issues, or a peer‑reviewed version that appeared later?

- Discussion: for “hot” topics, you can search title on X, blogs, Reddit to see if experts tore it apart or took it seriously.

If: years pass with no meaningful uptake or only “citation farms” → treat as low impact at best.

If you want this as something you can literally code into an SOP or checklist, you can define a small scoring rubric (0–2 points each) for the four dimensions: author signal, literature fit, argument quality, community signal. Papers that score low on multiple axes are “slop‑suspect”; ones that score high on at least the first three are “worth reading properly.”​​​
